Rubin (2001) Ho, Imai, King, and Stuart (2007) Angrist and Pischke (69-91) Kam and Palmer (2008) Henderson and Chatfield (2011) --- ## Mimic experiment by matching across covariates - Define treatment and control groups - Independence assumption - Achieved by randomization - Conditional ignorability - Not independence (which is not testable) - Trivial example with one covariate - Consequences - Covariate balance across treatment groups - Eliminate covariate non-overlap - Potentially discard a lot of observations - Is this a good thing or a bad thing? - Loss of representativeness - 1-to-1 or 1-to-k or k-to-k matching --- ## Problem of dimensionality - As number and levels of covariates increase, too many levels to match at - Solutions - CEM - Propensity score - Mahalanobis distance (we won't discuss this) --- ## Propensity score matching - Estimate a logistic regression model - Outcome: treatment variable - Predictors: covariates - Match on the fitted values, as a single covariate - Use either the logit scale or predicted probability - Benefits - Solves the dimensionality problem - Balancing covariates - Consequences - Model dependence! - No guarantee of covariate balance --- ## How do we use matched data? - Difference-in-means based on matched data - Weighted difference (ATT/ATC) - Regression on preprocessed data
